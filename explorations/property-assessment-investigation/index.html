The Question

Is Philadelphia's property tax system fair? Or does it systematically favor the wealthy at the expense of low-income homeowners?

Property taxes are the backbone of local government funding. In Philadelphia, they fund schools, police, fire departments, and infrastructure. The system is supposed to be simple: your home is assessed at its market value, and you pay a percentage of that value in taxes.

But what if the assessment system is broken? What if homes in poor neighborhoods are assessed at higher rates relative to their actual value than homes in wealthy neighborhoods?

That would mean low-income homeowners—many of them Black and Hispanic residents in historically underserved communities—are effectively subsidizing tax breaks for wealthy homeowners.

Why This Matters

Pennsylvania's Constitution contains a Uniformity Clause requiring that all taxes be applied equally. If the assessment system is systematically regressive, it may be unconstitutional—and affected homeowners could be entitled to refunds.

To answer this question, we analyzed every arm's-length property sale in Philadelphia from 2020 to 2024—over 116,000 transactions—and compared the sale prices (what homes actually sold for) to their assessed values (what the city claims they're worth).

The results are damning.

How We Measured Fairness

The International Association of Assessing Officers (IAAO) sets industry standards for property assessment quality. These aren't arbitrary benchmarks—they're the metrics used by courts, legislators, and oversight bodies to determine whether an assessment system is functioning properly.

Key Metrics Explained

Assessment Ratio

The ratio of assessed value to sale price. A ratio of 1.0 (100%) means the assessment perfectly matches market value. Above 1.0 = over-assessed. Below 1.0 = under-assessed.

Coefficient of Dispersion (COD)

Measures how much assessment ratios vary across properties. High COD means wildly inconsistent assessments. IAAO Standard: Below 15%

Price-Related Differential (PRD)

Measures whether cheap or expensive homes are systematically over/under-assessed. PRD > 1.03 indicates regressivity—cheap homes assessed higher relative to value. IAAO Standard: 0.98 - 1.03

Price-Related Bias (PRB)

A regression-based measure of how assessment ratios change with property value. Negative PRB indicates regressivity. IAAO Standard: ±0.03

The Money Chart: Who Pays More?

The clearest evidence of regressivity comes from plotting assessment ratios against sale prices. In a fair system, you'd see a flat horizontal line—all homes assessed at the same percentage of their value regardless of price.

Instead, we see a downward slope.

The pattern is unmistakable: as home values increase, assessment ratios decrease. Cheap homes cluster around and above the 100% "fair" line. Expensive homes sit well below it.

"For every 100% increase in sale price, the assessment ratio drops by 12.7 percentage points."

The Racial Dimension

Philadelphia's neighborhoods are highly segregated by race. Does the regressivity of the assessment system have a racial impact?

To answer this, we merged property sales data with Census demographic data at the census tract level, categorizing neighborhoods as "Majority Black" (60%+ Black), "Majority White" (60%+ White), or "Mixed."

The Disparity

The cheapest homes in majority-Black neighborhoods are assessed at 110% of their sale price—meaning they're valued ABOVE what they actually sold for. Meanwhile, the cheapest homes in majority-White neighborhoods are assessed at just 91%.

This translates to a 21% higher effective tax rate for Black homeowners at the bottom of the market.

The Nuance: Colorblind Algorithm, Disparate Impact

The assessment algorithm doesn't explicitly discriminate by race. But because:

  1. Black neighborhoods have lower median home values
  2. The algorithm over-assesses low-value homes
  3. Black residents are more likely to own low-value homes

...the system has disparate racial impact even without discriminatory intent. Under fair housing law, this may still constitute illegal discrimination.

Historical Trends: Getting Worse, Not Better

Using 13 years of data (308,464 sales from 2012-2024), we can track how the assessment system's fairness has evolved over time.

The Story in Four Acts

Act 1: Pre-2014 Baseline (2012-2013)

Before Philadelphia's "Actual Value Initiative" (AVI) reassessment, the regressivity gap was around 11%. Not great, but manageable. COD was high at 44-63%.

Act 2: The AVI Disaster (2014-2016)

The 2014 citywide reassessment was supposed to create fairness. Instead, the regressivity gap EXPLODED to 79-88%. Cheap homes were suddenly assessed at 150%+ of their sale prices while expensive homes stayed at 60%. PRD hit 1.25—catastrophically regressive.

Act 3: Gradual Recovery (2017-2021)

As market prices rose and assessments gradually adjusted, the gap narrowed to 13-22%. Still failing IAAO standards, but improving. This wasn't policy change—it was the market catching up to inflated assessments.

Act 4: Backsliding (2022-2024)

The gains are reversing. The regressivity gap has widened again to 34-36%. Cheap homes are once again being assessed at 115%+ of their sale prices. The system is getting worse, not better.

Legal Implications

Pennsylvania's Uniformity Clause

Article VIII, Section 1 of the Pennsylvania Constitution requires that "all taxes shall be uniform, upon the same class of subjects." Courts have interpreted this to require that property assessments be applied uniformly across all properties.

In 2021, a Philadelphia court already ruled that the city violated the Uniformity Clause by selectively reassessing commercial properties while ignoring residential ones. This analysis suggests the violations run much deeper.

IAAO Standards as Legal Benchmark

Courts and oversight bodies routinely use IAAO standards to evaluate assessment quality. A Coefficient of Dispersion above 15% is generally considered evidence of a non-uniform system. Philadelphia's COD of 34.7% is more than double that threshold.

Disparate Impact Under Fair Housing

Even without discriminatory intent, policies that disproportionately harm protected classes can violate fair housing law. The concentration of over-assessment in majority-Black neighborhoods could constitute disparate impact discrimination.

Potential Remedies

  • Class-action lawsuit seeking refunds for over-taxed homeowners
  • Court-ordered citywide reassessment
  • Legislative reform requiring regular assessment updates
  • Adoption of modern, transparent assessment methodology

Conclusion

"Philadelphia assesses cheap homes at nearly 100% of market value while expensive homes get assessed at only 77%—forcing the poor to subsidize the rich through a broken algorithm that fails every industry standard for fairness."

This investigation reveals a property tax system that is:

  • Regressive: Low-value homes pay higher effective tax rates than high-value homes
  • Non-uniform: Assessment ratios vary wildly, failing IAAO standards by a factor of two
  • Racially disparate: The cheapest homes in Black neighborhoods bear the heaviest burden
  • Technically incompetent: The city's model explains only 16% of market value
  • Potentially unconstitutional: May violate Pennsylvania's Uniformity Clause

The data doesn't lie. Philadelphia's property assessment algorithm is a wealth transfer mechanism—from the poor to the rich, from Black neighborhoods to white ones, from the vulnerable to the powerful.

It's time to fix it.

Methodology

Data Sources:

  • Real Estate Transfers: City of Philadelphia Department of Records (2020-2024)
  • Property Assessments: Office of Property Assessment (OPA) public dataset
  • Demographics: U.S. Census Bureau American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates (2022)

Sample: 308,464 arm's-length property sales (DEED documents) from 2012-2024 with valid assessments, filtered to sales between $25,000 and $5,000,000. Current year analysis focuses on 116,765 sales from 2020-2024.

Key Calculations:

  • Assessment Ratio = Assessed Value / Sale Price
  • COD = Average Absolute Deviation from Median / Median × 100
  • PRD = Mean Ratio / Weighted Mean Ratio (weighted by sale price)
  • PRB = Regression coefficient of ln(ratio) on ln(sale price)

Code and Data: Analysis performed using Python, DuckDB, and scikit-learn. All code and data available for replication.

Analysis conducted December 2024
